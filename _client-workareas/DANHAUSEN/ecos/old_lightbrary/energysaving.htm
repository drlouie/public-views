Improving Your Home Means Improving Your Energy Bill.

TOLEDO, Ohio, Sept. 24 /PRNewswire/ - Are you spending too much money each month on utility bills by wasting energy? The U.S. Department of Energy says that most Americans can reduce their utility bills by 10 to 50 percent by undertaking a few simple home improvement projects. Considering that the average household now spends more than $110 per month on energy, a relatively small investment can quickly pay significant dividends. Besides reducing your utility bills, an energy-efficient home is more comfortable and benefits the environment by requiring fewer fossil fuels to be burned, which produce greenhouse gas emissions that contribute to global warming.

10 Simple, Energy-Efficient, Do-It-Yourself Home Improvements.

"R" You Up to Code?
One of the best and easiest ways to save energy, cut heating and cooling costs, and make a home more comfortable is to add attic insulation. The Department of Energy's new guidelines recommend R-49 insulation (equivalent to 15 inches of fiber glass) for the attics of most U.S. homes. Because most attics only have 6 or 7 inches of insulation, adding a layer may help the majority of homeowners increase the energy efficiency of their homes. One product that makes the job of adding insulation easier for do-it-yourselfers is Owens Corning's virtually itch-free MIRAFLEX(R) R-25 (8-3/4-inch-thick) insulation. Contact the Energy Department for your area's specific R-values, which are determined by local heating/cooling costs and the climate. Also be sure to check the insulation in your exterior and basement walls, floors and crawl spaces to see if it meets the Department of Energy R-values recommended for your area (the higher the R-value, the greater the insulating power).


Plug the Drafts
Warm air leaking into your home during the summer and out of your home during the winter can waste energy. However, draft-proofing your home can cut energy costs by up to 10 percent, according to the Energy Department. The exterior of your house is your first line of defense against drafts, so caulk, seal and weather-strip around all seams, cracks and openings. Pay special attention around windows and where siding or bricks and wood trim meet. You can also reduce drafts from the inside of the house by caulking, sealing and weather-stripping around windows and door frames, and near electrical boxes and plumbing fixtures, all of which are likely locations for air leaks.


Window Wise
The energy wasted through poorly insulated windows and doors is equal to about as much energy as we obtain from the Alaska pipeline each year! If your windows rattle from the wind, or are swollen, rotted or drafty, consider replacing them with high-efficiency, easy to maintain double-pane vinyl windows or install storm windows over single-pane windows. Double- and triple-pane windows have insulated air- or gas-filled spaces between each pane, which resist heat flow.


Insulate Your Water Heater
Water-heating costs are the third largest energy expense in a home, typically accounting for about 14 percent of the utility bill. An old water heater that is warm to the touch usually needs additional insulation. Installing an insulation blanket around the water storage tank is an economical way to help make heating water more efficient. If you're purchasing a new water heater, an energy-efficient system will help reduce operating costs. Save additional money by lowering your water heater's temperature to 115 degrees F (140 degrees F if you use a dishwasher). And if you have an electric water heater, consider installing an electric water heater timer.


Check Your Equipment
Heating and cooling a home uses more energy than any other system in your home  typically 44 percent of your overall utility bill. Properly maintaining and adjusting existing equipment can improve energy efficiency by as much as 10 percent. Have your furnace and air ducts checked and serviced once a year, and change forced air heating system air filters each month to ensure maximum energy efficiency. Clean air registers, baseboard heaters and radiators as needed, and close vents in rooms used infrequently.


Shedding New Light
Another way to save money is to replace your home's incandescent light bulbs with high-efficiency compact fluorescent bulbs. According to the Department of Energy, replacing just 25 percent of incandescent bulbs in high- use areas with compact fluorescents could cut your lighting costs in half. Also make sure to turn off lights in rooms not being used, and "light zone" your home by concentrating lighting in reading and working areas as well as where it's needed for safety, such as in stairwells. Finally, install timers rather than leaving lights on continuously when you're away from home.


Shoot for the Stars
When shopping for a new appliance, look for the ENERGY STAR(R) label. These appliances have been identified by the Department of Energy and U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as the most energy-efficient products in their classes. Look for the ENERGY STAR label on dishwashers, air conditioners, windows, washing machines, refrigerators, thermostats, insulation, lighting fixtures, computers and homes. A new refrigerator with the ENERGY STAR label, for example, will save between $35 and $70 a year compared to the models designed 10 years ago, adding between $525 and $1,050 in savings over the average 15-20 year life of the unit.


Get with the Program
You can save up to 10 percent a year on heating and cooling bills by turning your thermostat back 10 to 15 percent for eight hours a day. You can do this automatically without sacrificing comfort by installing an automatic setback or programmable thermostat, which enables you to adjust the times you turn on the heating or air conditioning according to a preset schedule. As a result, you don't operate the equipment as much when you are asleep or when the house is unoccupied. Programmable thermostats can store and repeat multiple daily settings that can be overridden manually without affecting the rest of the program.


Slow the Flow
Make sure your kitchen sink faucet has an aerator and consider installing a low-flow shower head. By reducing the amount of water in the flow, you use less hot water and save the energy that would have been required to heat it. Moreover, the lower water pressure is barely noticeable.


Up on the Roof
Ventilation plays a large role in providing moisture control and reducing summer cooling bills. An improperly ventilated home can damage the structural wood and roofing system of the home and make living uncomfortable. Roof vents should be located along the entire ceiling cavity to help ensure proper airflow from the soffit to the attic. They're designed to help keep the air flow in your attic constant, reducing the level of developing moisture in the winter and venting hot air in the summer.
